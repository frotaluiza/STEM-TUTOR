## Summary: The Physics Explained

Across all five source documents, the consistent explanation for why MD works below the boiling point of water is:

1. **Boiling is not required for evaporation.** At any temperature above absolute zero, liquid water molecules at the surface have sufficient kinetic energy to escape into the vapor phase (evaporation). Boiling only occurs when the vapor pressure equals the ambient pressure. MD uses controlled evaporation at a liquid-vapor interface, not bulk boiling.

2. **The driving force is a vapor pressure gradient (delta-P_v), not boiling.** The temperature difference (delta-T) across the hydrophobic membrane creates an exponential difference in vapor pressure (per the Antoine equation / Clausius-Clapeyron relation). Water evaporates at the hot feed side interface (even at 40-80 degrees C, well below 100 degrees C) and the vapor diffuses through the membrane pores toward the lower-temperature, lower-vapor-pressure side.

3. **The Antoine equation mathematically describes how vapor pressure varies with temperature.** At 60 degrees C, the saturation vapor pressure of water is about 20 kPa (0.2 atm) -- enough to drive MD, though flux increases exponentially with temperature.

4. **MD is described as a "non-isothermal" process**, explicitly distinguishing it from boiling-based thermal distillation (MSF, MED) where water must be heated to its boiling point or flashed into steam.
