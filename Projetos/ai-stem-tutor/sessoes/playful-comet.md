## Summary of the core trade-off

The engineering trade-off between DCMD and AGMD can be summarized as follows from the sources:

| Aspect | DCMD | AGMD |
|--------|------|------|
| **Flux** | Higher (up to 4x higher in lab scale) | Lower (due to air gap mass transfer resistance) |
| **Conductive heat loss** | High (hot feed in direct contact with membrane; permeate in direct contact on cold side) | Low (stagnant air acts as thermal insulator) |
| **Thermal efficiency** | Low (high heat loss by conduction) | High (air gap reduces conductive losses) |
| **Temperature polarization** | More severe | Reduced (air gap insulates) |
| **Scale-up behavior** | Flux drops significantly at pilot scale | More stable; can outperform DCMD at pilot scale (~14% higher flux reported) |

The air gap is deliberately added despite adding mass transfer resistance because **it reduces conductive heat loss enough to improve overall thermal efficiency**, which becomes the dominant concern at pilot scale and in energy-cost-sensitive applications. The optimal operating point is a trade-off between membrane area (capital cost) and energy consumption (operating cost), as Alsaadi demonstrates with the Delta-Tcross optimization analysis.
